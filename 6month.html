  6 Month Pregnant: Symptoms and Fetal Development.



Yay! It's been six weeks,
  and huge changes are taking place,
  with things beginning to take shape.
  Your embryo has grown to the size of a pomegranate seed
  (approximately 0.08 to 0.2 inches) at 6 weeks pregnant.





  Symptoms of Pregnancy at Six Month Pregnant

Here's a rundown of what you might be going through in your sixth week of pregnancy:


  Spotting

  At six weeks, some mild spotting that doesn't even cover a pantyliner is completely normal.
  Most likely, it's implantation bleeding. However, if the spotting is excessively bloody
  and lasts more than two days, see your doctor immediately.


  Cramping

  At six weeks into your pregnancy, you may experience mild cramps.
  It's quite normal and indicates that your uterus and surrounding tissues are expanding
  to create place for your baby. However, if the pain is more severe than normal period cramps,
  or if it is accompanied by a fever or diarrhoea, see your doctor right away.


  Morning sickness is a common occurrence.

  If you're wondering why you haven't experienced morning sickness yet, it could happen this week. Even though it's called morning sickness, it can strike at any time of day, night, or evening. Certain scents, motions, an empty stomach, or nothing at all can make you feel sick. If you're feeling nauseous, keep some crackers or other starchy snacks on hand to help you feel better.


  Exhaustion

  Pregnancy exhaustion might leave you feeling absolutely fatigued, but that's perfectly normal. It's the rising levels of progesterone, a pregnancy hormone, that's making you feel weary. To combat tiredness, consider taking naps, eating small snacks, and doing some mild workouts. Make sure you're receiving enough iron, as a lack of it can induce anemia as well as fatigue.


  Urination on a regular basis

  You may have a stronger desire to use the restroom than usual. This occurs as a result of your kidneys working overtime to process the additional liquid in your body.


  Swings in mood

  Between now and the conclusion of your pregnancy, your emotions may be on a roller coaster. Mood swings are common in the first trimester and normally lessen in the second, but they may reappear during the third trimester. You can feel a little better by talking to loved ones, napping, eating healthy, and doing some light workouts.


  Constipation

  Increased progesterone levels can cause constipation because the hormone slows down the digestive system. To help with the condition, try exercising regularly, eating a balanced diet that includes high-fibre foods, and drinking at least eight glasses of water every day.
